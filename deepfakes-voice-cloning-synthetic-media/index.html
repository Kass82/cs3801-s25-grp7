Deepfake, Voice Cloning, & Synthetic Media

After scrolling through social media for a brief amount of time, it is pretty easy to come across a deepfake clip. Most of us see it as humorous, such as the clips of Obama playing with Joe Biden and Trump on Minecraft, which is obviously a deep fake video. However, not all such clips are fun and games. That is why today, Group 14 is going to go over deepfakes, voice cloning, and synthetic media, how it has harmed our community, as well as how the public is taking action against it.

What Are These Technologies?

To start, deepfakes, voice cloning, and synthetic media are terms used to describe video, images, and audio that have been artificially generated by artificial intelligence and/or machine learning models. Some examples include Computer Generated Imagery(CGI) in movie films to create monsters, cloning someone's voice to make them sing, and even generating different body parts onto other people. In the past, it was painfully obvious when these deepfakes were... fake... However, as time passes, artificial intelligence and machine learning are getting exponentially better, resulting in humans relying on artificial intelligence to tell them apart.

The Problem

Because these deepfakes are getting so realistic, they are starting to have real-world consequences for people. In the article, "Deepfakes and Synthetic Media: What are they and how are techUK members taking steps to tackle misinformation and fraud," they talk about many risks created by deepfakes, voice cloning, and synthetic media. Their biggest concerns were people being deepfaked and people being manipulated into believing deepfakes are real. For the former, there have been many instances of revenge pornography, where people are misusing artificial intelligence to create sexual content using the faces of friends, colleagues, or strangers and posting them online. While the latter, internal employees are being phished by scammers impersonating corporate executives, and politicians/businesses are being targeted, attempting to manipulate the media/market to ruin their reputation/brand.

Case Study: Taylor Swift

One case study involving the use of deepfake software for malicious intent is the Taylor Swift incident. According to the article, “Taylor Swift and the Dangers of Deepfake Pornography,” by the National Sexual Violence Research Center, the incident took place on January 24, 2024 in which sexually-explicit images of singer/songwriter Taylor Swift were shared across social media platforms, notably X. Though much of the images were promptly taken down from X’s platform, the emotional and psychological damage was already done. This incident reveals a large issue regarding the use of deepfake software for sexually explicit purposes which, according to the “Found through Google, bought with Visa and Mastercard: Inside the deepfake porn economy” by NBC News, accounts for 96% of the software’s use . This issue not only poses a significant risk towards influential figures, but also unsuspecting citizens who’s likeness is readily available to be used by these technologies.

Applying an Ethical framework

Our group came to the conclusion that deepfakes, voice cloning, and synthetic media are evolving too quickly and getting out of control. We agreed that the people designing these artificial intelligence and machine learning models, as well as the people using these models, should follow John Rawls' Veil of Ignorance approach. When developing/using these models, we need to keep in mind the harm they can bring to others, and that if other people were using these models, we would not want them to cause harm to us or our loved ones.

Takeaways

Some key takeaways to note are that there are actually some really good use cases for the technology behind these deepfakes. For example, also included in the article "Deepfakes and Synthetic Media: What are they and how are techUK members taking steps to tackle misinformation and fraud," is that the same technology is used for generating CGI/image assets for films and graphic designs, visualizing how furniture/homeware would look through Amazon's augmented reality, and allowing medical professionals to rehearse and practice procedures on virtual patients. Moreover, deepfake and voice cloning technologies can positively be used to preserve an individual’s likeness. For instance, artificial intelligence has been used to replicate, with permission, the voices of late actors such as James Earl Jones in Kenobi and Val Kilmer in Top Gun: Maverick. Furthermore, these case studies shine a positive light as to how deepfake/voice cloning softwares can be used as tools that provide value rather than stir controversies.
